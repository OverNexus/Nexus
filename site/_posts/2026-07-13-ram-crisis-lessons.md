## What's Happening
The 1980s were a transformative period for technology, with the rise of personal computers and the dawn of the gaming industry. However, this growth was not without its challenges. The RAM crisis, which occurred during this time, was characterized by a severe shortage of random access memory (RAM), a critical component in computer hardware. This shortage was exacerbated by the limited manufacturing capacity of semiconductor companies and the soaring demand for computers. The situation was further complicated by the oil crisis, which affected the global economy and had a ripple effect on various industries, including technology. On November 12, 1982, IBM announced the IBM PC/370, which required significant amounts of RAM, further straining the already tight supply.

The RAM crisis had far-reaching consequences, impacting not only the tech industry but also the broader economy. Companies like Intel and Samsung, which were major players in the semiconductor market, struggled to meet the demand for RAM. This led to significant price increases, making computers and other electronic devices more expensive for consumers. The crisis also spurred innovation, as companies began to explore alternative technologies and manufacturing processes to alleviate the shortage. For instance, the development of more efficient memory technologies, such as dynamic RAM (DRAM), helped to mitigate the effects of the crisis. By understanding the historical context of the RAM crisis, we can better appreciate the complexities of the modern tech industry and the importance of adaptive innovation in the face of challenges.

The significance of the RAM crisis extends beyond the 1980s, as it offers valuable lessons for modern tech companies and policymakers. From a technical perspective, the RAM crisis was driven by the limitations of semiconductor manufacturing at the time. The production of RAM required significant amounts of silicon, which was in short supply due to the limited capacity of fabrication plants. The process of manufacturing semiconductors was also complex and time-consuming, involving multiple stages of design, production, and testing. To address the shortage, companies began to explore new manufacturing technologies, such as the use of larger wafers and more efficient production processes. These innovations helped to increase the yield of functional chips per wafer, reducing the cost and increasing the availability of RAM.
